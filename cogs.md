# Attentional blink
- revisiting last lecture, inattentional blindness is when you miss stimulus that you are not paying attention to
- attentional blink, by contrast, is when you miss a stimulus due to the fact you are paying attention to it
- attentional blink: the second of two targets cannot be detected or identified when it appears close in time to the first
- tested in rapidly flashing letters, find the red letter
	- attention is primed to find a red letter
	- if you find the first letter, you will miss the second red letter that comes milliseconds after
		- if you miss the first red target, you will have a normal chance of seeing the second red target
	- somehow, successfully directing attention to the first target causes a brief "blink" in perception
# Dichotic listening
- participant wears headphones that play two separate audiobooks for each ear
	- participant told to recite only one of the audiobooks
	- they are successful in tuning out the other audiobook, and they are still consciously aware that there is something playing, just not what its saying
- aims to answer the question: at what level of processing does attention begin to work?
	- at what point does attention cull stimulus
	- at what point does attention promote stimulus
- based on the experiment, unattended audiobook stimulus reaches the conscious experience, but not high enough on the conscious processing that we can understand what the audiobook is saying
- We will see later that another version of this experiment shows a different story
# Broadbent's filter model
- an early attempt to model exactly where attention begins to work
- runs into some problems, but a good basis for theory
- stimulus begins at the sensory organ -> selective filter (attention) -> higher level processing -> short term memory
	- attention theoretically culls unattended stimulus before it reaches higher level processing
- the proposed method of filtering was based on physical properties of the stimulus
	- so our attention would filter stimulus based on things like loudness, color, etc.
# Cocktail party effect
- the ability that people have to selectively attend to one stimulus in a sea of noise
	- yet, we are able to hear our own name among the sea of noise, despite previously tuning the noise out
- being able to process one's name requires higher processing
- presents major complications for the Broadbent filter model
	- Broadbent filter model puts the filter before higher processing 
	- but if you can pick out your name from the noise, then that means the noise made it to higher processing at some point
		- if the filter truly blocked unattended stimulus before it made it to higher processing, then people could not have even processed their name in the first place
	- therefore, the unattended stimulus has reached higher level processing
# Dichotic listening expanded
- in an updated experiment, once again participants wear headphones with two audios playing separately in each ear
- this time, the individual audio tracks are incoherent one their own
- instead, the two tracks are a mashup
	- track 1: Dear 7 Jane
	- track 2: 8 Aunt 9
- When participants are told to attend only to track 1, they will say they heard "dear aunt Jane", which is wrong; the right answer is "dear 7 Jane"
- "dear aunt Jane" makes more sense, but the "aunt" came from track 2, which is supposedly completely tuned out
- therefore, the unattended stimulus has reached higher level processing
# Treisman's Attenuation model
- attention does not completely cull unattended stimulus
- rather, it grades all stimulus by importance
- important stimulus are processed with more power than trivial stimulus
# Never processed or quickly forgotten?
- it is widely believe now that even unattended stimulus reaches higher processing
- but why do we not consciously perceive the higher level details of the unattended stimulus
- currently, there are two beliefs
	- unattended stimulus reaches higher processing, but never reaches consciousness due to a lack of processing
	- unattended stimulus reaches both higher processing AND consciousness, but does not reach short-term memory, and we can not remember the stimulus
- we have no way to tell if we have forgotten something or if it never happened
